Section 04 · The Trojan Horse

How They Infiltrated MAGA

In 2016, AIPAC publicly opposed Trump and urged members to abstain. By 2024, they were his single largest outside funding source. This was not a change of heart. It was a strategic decision by a highly organized group that plays long-ball politics: if the outsider wins anyway, you get inside him before he understands what happened.

🏈 Football Analogy

Imagine the opposing team's offensive coordinator spent four years publicly trashing your quarterback — calling him dangerous, erratic, unpresidential. Then, when he won the championship, that same coordinator suddenly started sending his best players to your locker room, funding your training facility, and installing his assistants as your team's coaches. You'd want to know why. That's what happened with AIPAC and Trump.

In 2016, AIPAC's annual policy conference invited Donald Trump to speak. When he did, many AIPAC members walked out in protest. The organization issued statements distancing itself from his "divisive rhetoric." The Republican Jewish Coalition, an AIPAC ally, was deeply uncomfortable with Trump's "America First" framing — which they rightly understood as skeptical of foreign entanglements, including unconditional support for Israel.

The Israel lobby spent much of the 2016 cycle either passively sitting out the presidential race or quietly hoping Trump would lose. They didn't need to spend heavily — they had Hillary Clinton, a reliable ally.

"Sheldon Adelson is looking to give big dollars to Rubio because he feels he can mold him into his perfect little puppet."

— Donald Trump, 2015, correctly identifying how AIPAC donors operate

Trump won anyway — without their blessing. That changed everything.

After 2016, the Israel lobby made a calculated, unsentimental decision: get inside the MAGA movement before the MAGA movement figures out what the lobby actually is. This is a classic play from a community that has practiced political survival for centuries — when an outsider wins power unexpectedly, you don't fight him, you fund him, flatter him, and fill his cabinet with your people before he fills it himself.

The Republican Jewish Coalition, an AIPAC ally, spent over $14 million supporting Trump between 2020 and 2024. Miriam Adelson contributed $120 million to Trump's 2024 campaign. The same organizations that were uncomfortable with "America First" in 2016 — because America First is, by definition, not Israel First — became his largest financial backers the moment he proved he could win without them.

This is what highly organized, tribally cohesive political communities do. They do not waste energy opposing power. They absorb it. Every other ethnic and cultural group in America would benefit from studying this playbook — because right now, only one group is running it at scale.
